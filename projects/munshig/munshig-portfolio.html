The trigger

"Month end tak paise kahan chale jate hain, pata hi nahi chalta."

That line came up again and again in conversations with friends, colleagues, and family who'd recently started earning. Spending felt fine in the moment — the trouble only showed up at the end of the month, when the balance was low and the next salary hadn't landed yet.

A cycle that kept repeating, salary after salary — not from a lack of intent, but from how hard it was to keep the habit going.

Reframing the problem

The question wasn't "why don't people track expenses" — it was why the habit never lasted.

That shift moved the project away from building another expense manager, toward understanding what actually breaks the habit of tracking.

Conventional framing

People do not track their expenses.

→
Reframed problem

People struggle to sustain the habit of expense tracking.

The problem was tracking.
The problem was interaction friction.

Design principles

Four rules that shaped every decision after this point

Before sketching any solution, these became the filter every idea had to pass through.

01

No new behaviour

Fit into habits people already have, rather than asking them to adopt a new workflow.

02

Minimal cognitive load

No categories to pick, no forms to think through — just say what you spent.

03

Near-zero interaction cost

Recording an expense should feel as light as sending a text message.

04

Transparency & ownership

People should always have direct access to and ownership of their own financial records.

Choosing the platform

Why a chatbot, and why Telegram

Natural language made conversational logging viable — so the format could simply borrow how people already talk to each other.

Dedicated mobile app Web app Spreadsheet workflow Messaging platform — chosen
  • People were already comfortable with messaging as an interaction.
  • No new app to build, download, or install.
  • Bot creation and deployment were free and fast to set up.
  • Backend integration was straightforward to wire up.
  • The platform allowed quick experimentation and iteration.
  • The goal was accessibility first, not maximum functionality.

Alpha testing

Testing with a small group of friends and family

A short guide introduced the bot and walked testers through onboarding. The goal wasn't only "does it work" — it was "does this actually change behaviour."

What testing looked at

  • Whether people kept logging expenses over time, not just on day one
  • Where friction showed up in the conversation flow
  • Which parts of onboarding felt unclear or clunky
  • Features testers expected but didn't find
  • Whether chatting felt more sustainable than a traditional app

What came out of it

Friction beats features

Most people already know tracking helps. The real barrier is making it sustainable, not adding more capability.

Existing habits are assets

Building inside a habit people already have beats asking them to learn a new one.

Feature-rich ≠ best

How naturally something fits a routine matters more than how much it can do.

Reframing comes first

This stayed an expense project on paper, but became a study in reducing behavioural friction in practice.

Reflection

MunshiG began as a personal attempt to fix something I kept running into myself. Working through it taught me to question the first version of a problem statement, and to look harder for whatever is quietly preventing a good habit from sticking.

What came out the other end wasn't just a Telegram bot for logging expenses — it was a small, practical exercise in using design to remove friction, so that a better everyday habit could actually hold.
